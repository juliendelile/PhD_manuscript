3.1.1. Soft material no physics available

The "Entwicklungsmechanik" introduced the importance of mechanics in the study of developmental systems. From that point on, various theoretical models have been proposed.

The purpose of these models is to propose mechanical explanation sufficient to generate the kinematic behavior of the cellular tissue.

The diversity of theoretical models is justified by the very evasive nature of multicellular systems compared to the classical object of study in physics. Classical physics has developed framework adapted to objects which present spatio-temporal regularities, however the living matter is characterized by its behavioral heterogeneity. Indeed, the cell behaviors may rapidly change over time as its molecular structure is perpetually reorganizing itself.

This heterogeneity of behavior induces that some type of physical models are more relevant to describe some aspects of cell dynamics than others (see discussion interfacial tension vs protrusion).

Cells behave neither as a solid or a fluid, or as both at once. Cellular tissue are often described as "soft matter".

A fundamental notion to describe cell behavior is its ability to convert stored energy into mechanical work. This creates nonequilibrium states which deviate from classical equilibriua. This notion, which fund the field of "active matter", is applied at various level of organization, whether it is complex macromolecules, cells from a developing embryo, or even flocking birds . In the vocabulary associated with these object, the qualification "self"-(...ing) or "spontaneous" ... conveys the notion of autonomous action independent from an external influence. In egg embryos, the energy necessary to the motion of the cells originate from the yolk and in mammmals, the energy is provided by the mother but as food energy and not as an external mechanical action.

Moreover, this notion is the direct consequence of macroscopic scaling and their approximated rules. At the microscopic scale, inter-atomic potential are still respecting equilibrium and there is no notion of active and passive action anymore.

intéressant A continuity equation in physics is an equation that describes the transport of a conserved quantity. Since mass, energy, momentum, electric charge and other natural quantities are conserved under their respective appropriate conditions, a variety of physical phenomena may be described using continuity equations. Continuity equations are a stronger, local form of conservation laws. For example, it is true that "the total energy in the universe is conserved". But this statement does not immediately rule out the possibility that a lot of energy could disappear from Earth while simultaneously appearing in another galaxy. A stronger statement is that energy is locally conserved: Energy can neither be created nor destroyed, nor can it "teleport" from one place to another—it can only move by a continuous flow. A continuity equation is the mathematical way to express this kind of statement.

"Solid mechanics" focuses on the study of the response of solid matter to the action of forces and/or thermal loading.

The most important theoretical tool used in solid mechanics is the tensor. The tensor is a 3-dimensional matrix which represents an ellipsoid whose axes convey the principal directions and amplitude of the gradient of a quantity in a local neighborhood.

The relevant quantities are the strain and the stress. The strain expresses the deformation of the solid, it is deduced from the displacement of the solid. The stress is a measure of the internal forces acting inside the solid as a reaction to an external action, and its dimension is a force per unit area (see figure XXX). The relation between the stress tensor and the strain tensor establishes the mechanical properties of the matter.

Fluid mechanics

Fluid mechanics focus on the study of fluids (liquids, gases and plasmas) and the responses to forces applied on them.

As a part of the continuum mechanics, the fluids properties of density, pressure, temperature or velocity is assumed to be continuous.

Fundamental hypotheses :

  • conservation of mass : the rate of change of mass contained in a control volume (a cube, a sphere...) is equal to the rate of mass entering the control surface minus the rate of mass going out of it.
  • conservation of energy
  • conservation of momentum
  • continuity: the continuity equation assures that the aforementioned quantities (mass, energy, momentum) are not only conserved but also locally conserved as they can only displace as a continuous flow. This gives to modeled fluids their apparent smoothness.

The principal equation ruling fluid behavior is the Navier-Stokes equation. It describes the motion of the fluid as a function of the balance of forces acting in it. Its general form for the conservation of the momentum is written:

$$ {\partial{\bf v}\over{\partial t}} + ({\bf v} \cdot \nabla) {\bf v} = {1\over\rho} \nabla \mathbb{P} + {1\over\rho} {\bf F} $$

where

  • \({\bf v}\) is the velocity vector,
  • \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid,
  • \(\mathbb{P}\) is a tensor which describes the normal and tangential stresses applied on a fluid particle,
  • \({\bf F}\) is a body force vector (force per unit volume).

Additional conditions on the fluid proprieties allow to determine a solvable set of equations. If the fluid is incompressible, the divergence of the stress term \(\nabla \mathbb{P}\) is replaced by the sum of a pressure gradient \(- \nabla p\) for the normal component of the stress and, for a Newtonian fluid, a dynamic viscosity constant \(\mu\) times the Laplacian of the velocity \(\nabla^2{\bf v}\) expresses the tangential component of the stress.

Unfortunately, in biological material, the Newtonian assumption is hardly satisfying as the viscosity model is not linear. The use of more complicated non-linear stress tensor is required and under particular external conditions, hole may appear in the continuum. However, a simplifying assumption adapted to biological material can be made as the inertial forces (LHS of the Navier-Stokes equation) are negligible compared to the forces of viscosity (RHS of NV). The Stokes equation express this assumption:

$$ \nabla \mathbb{P} + {\bf F} = 0 $$

Only the simplest forms of Navier-Stokes equation are analytically solvable (Poiseuille flow, Couette flow, Taylor-Green vortex... (ajouter figure avec ces solutions ?? XXXX)), more complex conditions require the use of computer simulations (Computational Fluid Dynamics - CFD).

Cellular Potts model

The cellular Potts model is one of the most successful framework to study multicellular assembly. It was introduced by F. Graner and J. Glazier as an adaptation of the Potts model to reproduce the cell sorting behavior proposed by Steinberg .

Historically, the Potts model derives from the Ising model which was invented by Wilhelm Lenz in 1920. Ising model describes the phase transition of the magnetic dipole moments of atomic spins arranged on a lattice (taken a value of +1 or -1). For example, it allows the characterization of the transition between the ferromagnetic and the paramagnetic phases. Spins only interacts with their immediate neighbors on the lattice (local principle). The coupling between the spins is quantified by an energy function called the Hamiltonian. The Hamiltionian is equal to the sum over every atoms of the coupling factors between an atom's spin and its neighbors' spin. The aligned neighbors spins have a lower energy of coupling than anti-aligned neighbors spins. However, the Ising model presents a static analysis of the transition and not a dynamical evolution of the spins value . In 1943, Ashkin and Teller added dynamics by using a Monte-Carlo algorithm which updates the values of all spins based on the Boltzmann probability.

In 1952, Renfrey Potts extended the Ising model by allowing the spin to take multiples values. More spin coupling had to be define but the Hamiltonian function remained the same. The Metropolis algorithm, which iteratively selects randomly a spin, and updates its value according to the evolution of the Hamiltonian if the spin value is changed, allows smoother transition based on a principle of locality. With the Metropolis algorithm, the Potts became a kinetic simulation and allows the study of various systems from metal grains to foam . The cellular Potts model applies the model to cells by stating that a cell is represented by an ensemble of neighbor grid point and the "spin" of each grid point is the id of the cell. Its first version used an Hamiltonian with a neighbor coupling term representing the different adhesion coefficient according to cell types and a volume constraining term to penalize the variation of cell size.

The cellular Potts model was later extended with P. Hogeweg to allow the integration of various cell behaviors: cell growth, cell division, cell migration and a modulation term was introduced in the Hamiltonian to take into account the rigidity of the cell through a surface conserving law. Its name became the Glazier-Graner-Hogeweg model (GGH model).

3.1.2. Particle-based physics

In particle-based physics models, each element of matter is represented by a particle.

Mechanical properties of the system are the parameters of the interaction potentials between neighbor particles.

Describing a particle-based model can be organized in two parts, resumed by the following questions:

  • how to divide up the physical space into particles ? (Space)
  • how to relate the motion of the particles with their mechanical properties ? (Forces)

Spatial neighborhood

Equations ruling particle system dynamics always used a term \( \sum\limits_{\text{neighbors j of i}}^{} \). The aim of this part is to describe how these sets of neighbors are determined.

In off-lattice simulation, particle are free to take any coordinate in the Euclidian space \(\mathbb{R}^{3}\). Neighborhood is not implicit any more and algorithm are needed to connect neighbor particles. For the following, let \(P \) be the set of \(n\) particles \(p_i \) with position vector \(x_i\).

All to all

Every particle are interconnected. \(\frac{n (n-1)}{2}\) bidirectional connections in between the swarm of particles \(P\).

$$N_{\text{all to all}} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix}$$

Selection by metric criteria (distance based)

Particles interaction often decreases in intensity when their relative distance increases. At some point, this interaction may even be neglected compared to interaction with closer particle. A first definition may be given: a pair of particles is considered neighbor if they interact with a non null intensity. Each particle generates a local field of potential interaction and particle out of this local field are not considered as neighbor any more. The \({N}_{\text{all to all}}\) connection matrix became a sparser one.

$$N_{\text{sparser}} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix}$$

In its simplest form, this locality principle may be captured by a intrinsic size parameter \(s\), applied to all particle of \(P\). It implies that the local domain is defined by a sphere of radius \(f(s)\). In the Euclidean space, it means each particle \(p_i\) and \(p_j\) are considered neighbors if their distance is less than \(f(s)\). $$ \left \| x_i - x_j \right \| \leq f(s) $$ If particle have spheroidal shapes, a intrinsic parameter \(r_i\) may be associated to each particle \(p_i\). The metric criteria may be written: $$ \left \| x_i - x_j \right \| \leq f(r_i,r_j) $$ More complex metric scenarii may be considered if more than one intrinsic parameter is associated with the particle (for example an ellipsoidal domain). Theses cases will not be developed here.

Selection by topological criteria

Some models direct the use of more refined neighborhood criteria. Distance only criteria are not sufficient.

This is the case when spheroidal approximation is not sufficient.

This is the case when particle represent a physical piece of the space which is not a neglected compared to the volume of the local interaction field of the particle.

We introduce \( \phi \) the ratio of physical volume of the object represented by a particle and the volume of the domain of its local interaction field.

On one side, planets and their gravitation field or atoms and their "atomic" potential have a ratio which tends to zero.

On the other side, when object interacts with other object through surface contact as it is the case in living cell system, the ratio close to 1.

see Knudsen number http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knudsen_number

For high \(\phi\) system, some criteria may define more specifically what part of the space is occupied by the object associated with the particle.

For systems where objects are closely packed along each others, Voronoï diagram \(V(P)\)is a convenient solution to determine a decomposition of the space into convex polyhedra associated to particles \(p_i \in P\). Such a convex polyhedron is called a Voronoï cell. It is defined as follows:

$$ V(p_i) = \{ x \in \mathbb{R}^{3} : \forall j \in \left [ 0,n-1\right]\setminus i, \left \| x-x_i \right \| \leq \left \| x-x_j\right \| \} $$

Attraction/Repulsion potential

Every particle-based model interaction potential share common features: repulsion at short distance, until an equilibrium distance \(r_{eq}\) and attraction afterwards, until the border of the interaction field, whether it is finite or not (see Spatial Neighborhood.)

Equilibrium distance

Two distinctive characterization of distance equilibrium co-exists:

  • the equilibrium distance is implicitly obtain by the relaxed state of the dynamical system. It will be function of the material properties (elasticity, adhesion ...).
  • the equilibrium distance is explicitly specified by geometric calculus.

Implicit equilibrium distance

As soon as two cells touch each other, adhesive forces at their surface tends to augment the contact area until inner resistance compensate them totally. This implicitly defines an equilibrium surface. The most influential field of research for describing this phenomenon is contact mechanics. Initiated by the publication of Heinrich R. Hertz papers "On the contact of elastic solids" (1881) and "On the Contact of Rigid Elastic Solids and on Hardness" (1882), contact mechanics successfully relates the deformation of two elastic bodies which touch each other to the load applied and the mechanical properties of the bodies. Originally, Hertz only described the non adhesive surface case but two concurrent models (Johnson-Kendall-Roberts and Derjaguin-Muller-Toporov) added adhesion related terms in the equation, each performing better on different material.

For example, if two elastic sphere with Elastic modulii \(E_i\) and \(E_j\), Poisson's ratios \(\nu_i\) and \(\nu_j\) and radii \(R_i\) and \(R_j\), their contact is a circle of radius \(a\). The JKR model establishes a relation between load \(F_{JKR}\) and contact radius by:

$$ F_{JKR} (a) = \frac{4 E^{*} a^3}{3 R^{*}} - 2 \sqrt{ 2 \pi E^{*} w_{adh} a^3} $$

with \( E^{*} = \left ( \frac{1-{\nu_i}^2}{E_i} + \frac{1-{\nu_j}^2}{E_j} \right )^{-1} \), \( R^{*} = \left ( \frac{1}{R_i} + \frac{1}{R_j} \right )^{-1} \) and \(w_{adh}\) the adhesive energy per unit area.

The equilibrium surface radius \(a_{eq}\) is obtained for \(F_{JKR} = 0\):

$$ a_{eq} = \left ( \frac{9 \pi w_{adh} {R^{*}}^2}{2 E^{*}} \right )^{1/3} $$

As the shape of the deformation of the surface is assumed to be parabolic, the equilibrium distance between the two spheroid particle centers can also be inferred.

These equations have been used in multicellular modeling. But in addition with some assumption made which could be discussed (friction-less surface, the deformation has to be small compared with the sphere radii), the main drawback is that all these theories are designed for two interacting spheroid particle. When a particle is surrounded by multiple particles, the free surface surrounding the contact area is occupied by other contact area, limiting its augmentation or amplifying its diminution.

The particular problem tends to decrease the cellular volume when the interfacial tension increases (see figure above). This annihilates the advanced characterization of the surface equilibrium by contact mechanics models. Some have added a volume consevation term to correct this tendency, but the outcome is also a perturbation of contact mechanics hypothesis.

The general problem is that the variable used is the particle center position, and there is no explicit usage of the surface with for example the Voronoï vertices. Pair of neighbor particles expressed in the \( \sum\limits_{\text{neighbors j of i}}^{} \) term does not allow to design more adapted models. This is why we simplify the distance equilibrium and use an explicit definition.

3.2.1. Equation of Motion

The equation of motion is the core of every mechanical model. It relates the spatial variables of the model and the time through hypothesized laws reflecting our understanding of the biomechanical properties of the studied phenomenon. In classical mechanics, the famous Newton's laws are the fundamental principle on which the equation of motion is build. In the following section, we will explain how an adaptation of Newton's laws is needed.

Newton's second law states that the acceleration \(\vec{a}\) of a body is proportional to the sum of the forces applied to it \(\vec{F}\). The proportionality factor is the inverse of the mass of the body, a quantity assumed to remain constant over time.

$$ m \vec{a} = \vec{F} $$

In particle-based physics, each particle \(p_i\) (mass \(m_i\), position \(X_i\), velocity \(v_i\), acceleration \(a_i\), size \(R_i\)) are related by the Newton's law straightforwardly:

$$m_i \vec{a_i} = \vec{F}(X_i, v_i, R_i, ...)$$

However, the physical world in which cells evolve is a very counterintuitive one. Cells are so small and their interaction are so sticky that the physics of their motion is radically different from the one we are accustomed to. The main difference is the disappearance of inertial forces.

In "Life at low Reynolds number" , E.M. Purcell explains how a water-immersed cell stops moving as soon as he stops pushing it. The cell, an E. Coli bacteria, is so small that the viscous forces exerted by the surrounding water are overwhelming the inertial forces due to the motion.

When an object of characteristic length \(R\) moves at a velocity \(v\) in a fluid of density \(\rho\) and dynamic viscosity \(\mu\), the quantity that measures the ratio of the inertial forces to the viscous forces is called the Reynolds number \(Re\). This dimension-less number is expressed as the following:

$$Re = \frac{\rho v R}{\mu}$$

The ratio \(\frac{\mu}{\rho}\) is the kinematic viscosity \(\nu\) and it has a value of \( 10^{-6} {m}^2 {s}^{-1} \) for water.

For a man of characteristic length \(1m\) swimming at \(1 m s^{-1}\), \(Re\) is about \(10^6\). For a bacterium of characteristic length \(10^{-6} m\) swimming at \(10^{-5} m s^{-1}\), \(Re\) is \(10^{-5}\). The physics of inertia and viscosity is totally different for these two examples. The balance shifts from inertial forces for a man to the viscosity forces for a cell.

A more striking example is the coasting distance of the same bacterium when it stops self-propulsing in water. When only inertial and viscous and inertial are at work, Newton's second law is written:

$$m \frac{dv}{dt} = - \gamma_{drag} v$$

The solution is \(v(t) = v_0 e^{ -\frac{\gamma_{drag}}{m} t}\) (see Introductory Biophysics - Perspectives on the living state p.96 by J. Claycomb and J. Tran)

Integrating it from \(t=0\) to \(t=\inf\), gives the coasting distance of the bacterium \(d\):

$$d = \frac{v_0 m}{\gamma_{drag}}$$

For a bacterium swimming at \(10^{-5} m s^{-1}\), this distance is \(0.1\) angstrom.

This simple calculus confirms that if the bacterium is not moving by itself or pushed by an external actor, it stops “immediatly” its motion.

Purcell interprets theses results as the following: “If you are at very low Reynolds number, what you are doing at the moment is entirely determined by the forces that are exerted on you at the moment, and by nothing in the past.”

This implies that in these low Reynolds environments, applied forces produce a velocity, not an acceleration. The displacement is proportional the instantaneous force and inversely proportional to the a damping coefficient \(\lambda\)

Theses results thereby allow a bypass of Newton's Second Law and we write the new equation of motion that will be used in our model :

$$\lambda \vec{v} = \vec{F}$$

The previous argument has been evaluated for a solid object moving in a fluid flow. In a multicellular environment as encountered in developing systems, we assume that the aforementioned principle remains the same because:

  • the dimension of the cells are similar to the aforementioned bacterium (from 300 microns for the zygote to 0.3 microns at stage 1024 cells, 0.02 microns when the embryo is composed of about 15000 cells),
  • the density is equivalent.
  • the kinematic viscosity of the surrounding cells taken as a flow can be considered greater than the one of water. For example, at 18°C, the kinematic viscosity of ketchup is 50000 times higher than the one of water. (see Odell et al. The mechanical basis of morphogenesis. I. Epithelial folding and invagination. Developmental Biology (1981) Appendix 2 for a similar justification)

For each particle \(p_i\), its motion is governed by its interactions with neighbor cells:

$$\lambda_i \vec{v}_{i} = \sum_{neighbors\;j} \vec{F}_{i,j}$$

The main difference with the example of a solid object is that the surrounding cells are responsible for the damping of the motion but also for the motion itself. Here there is no hand pushing the cell like Purcell explained, the pushing is executed by every cell intrinsic behavior.

This is why these interactions are decoupled in the following way:

  • the damping coefficient \(\lambda\) assure the global damping of the particle. (It plays an equivalent role as the mass in Newton's Second Law). This weighting coefficient is proportional to the surface of the cell: \( \lambda_i = \lambda_0 {R_i}^2 \) (show study of \(R^0\), \(R^1\), \(R^2\)).
  • A passive force \(\vec{F_P}\) of interaction between neighbor cell maintains the integrity of the cellular volume and controls both the stiffness and the adhesion of their interaction. This force is always exerting its action whatever the state of the cell. However, it is modulated through its stiffness and adhesion.
  • An active force \(\vec{F_A}\) of interaction between neighbor cell executes specific behavior like protrusive activity or apical constriction. The force depends of the state of the cell and it disappears when the cell is in a "resting" state.

The weight, exerted by earth gravitational field, derived from Newton's law of universal gravitation \(W_i = m_i \frac{G M_{earth}} {{R_{earth}}^2} \) is neglected.

In conclusion, here is the final equation we will use throughout all the study:

$$\vec{v}_{i} = \frac{1}{\lambda_i} \sum_{neighbors\;j} \left ( \vec{F_P}_{i,j} + \vec{F_A}_{i,j} \right )$$

The following section 3.2.2. will detail how are defined the neighbors \(p_j\) of the each particle \(p_i\) and the section 3.2.3. will focus on the forces between theses particles.

3.2.2. Spatial Neighborhood

Cell sustains forces from the cells which are in contact with them. The notion of contact is straightforward in some modeling paradigm (potts for example) but in highly abstracted model like particle-based model, specific algorithm are to be devised.

At each time step, the only information about the spatial structure is that each cell, represented by a spheroidal particle \(p_i\) belonging to a swarm \(S\), has a position \(X_i (x_i,y_i,z_i)\), and a volume \(V_i\) (see figure on the left). The objective of this section is to infer these intrinsic parameters the neighboring links and contact surface area (figure on the right). We will present a two steps strategy: a first selection of potential neighbors with a metric criteria and a refinement of this list according to topological consideration.

The regular Delaunay triangulation is in many way the most adapted method, it establishes a list of neighbors according to topological criteria dependent of each particle size (see state of the art). However, its drawback is its computational heaviness. We have adopted a parallel algorithmic structure exploiting the computational power of GPGPU (see relevant part), and, to our knowledge, there is no parallel implementation for regular triangulation yet.

We designed a new parallel neighboring building strategy (see algorithm part for implementation) which insure that a topologically plausible structure is realized.

A comparison between the regular triangulation and our neighboring strategy is proposed at the end of this part.

Our method consecutively builds two lists of neighbor particles. The first selection \(\mathcal{N}_m (p_i)\), based on metric criteria, is filtered by a topological criteria to define the final selection of neighbors \(\mathcal{N}_t (p_i)\).

Metric selection of neighbor particles

Two cells of radius \(R_i\), \(R_j\) and of position \(X_i\) and \(X_j\) are considered neighbor according to the metric criteria iff their relative distance is less than a constant factor \(c_{max}\) times the sum of their radii \(R_i+R_j\).

We denote by \(r_{max}\) this cutoff value: \(r_{max} = c_{max} (R_i+R_j)\).

The set of neighbor particle of a particle \(p_i\) according to the metric criteria is defined by the following:

$$\mathcal{N}_m (p_i) := \left \{ p_j \in S : \left \| X_i - X_j \right \| \leq c_{max} \cdot (R_i+R_j) \right \} $$

Even if the particles are considered "spheroidal", we incorporate the ability to deform to access farer neighbor cells. \(r_{max}\) sets the maximum distance of this deformation. The sphere of radius \(r_{max}\) represents the domain of space where the particle can potentially interact.

More generally, we need to define a law that relates the distance between two neighbor particles to the area of the contact surface. \(r_{max}\) is the limit from which this area is equal to zero.

Empirical quantification of the surface of contact

In the absence of real experimental data, we generate an artificial construction based on the weighted delaunay triangulation. This will allow us to infer an empirical law which approximate the surface-distance relationship in this articifial case and, by extension, to the modeled multicellular system.

An ellipsoidal domain of space is filled with particles belonging to three consecutive cell generation \(g1\), \(g2\), \(g3\) in equal proportion. Their volume are \(V_{g1}\), \(V_{g2}= \frac{1}{2} V_{g1}\), \(V_{g3} = \left ( \frac{1}{2} \right )^{2} V_{g1}\). Similarly, the particle being considered spheroidal, their radii are \(R_{g1}\), \(R_{g2} = \left ( \frac{1}{2} \right )^{\frac{1}{3}} R_{g1}\), \(R_{g3} = \left ( \frac{1}{2} \right )^{\frac{2}{3}} R_{g1}\). These radii are the weighing coefficient of the triangulation.

The number \(N\) of each particle generation is determined to fit the total volume occupied by the \(3N\) particles with the volume of the ellipsoid.

The ellipsoid self deforms to impose a rearrangement of the particles, generating various spatial reorganisation. The first semi-principal axis of the ellipsoid is cycling sinusoidallly, the second remains constant and the third is specified to maintain the volume of the ellipsoid constant.

On the figure above, only the close Voronoï volume that does not belong to the convex hull are shown. The color code highlights the normalized difference of volume between the theoretical volume of the cell and the volume specified by the weighted Voronoï \( c = \frac{V_{obs} - V_{theo}}{V_{theo}} \).

When two neighbor cells both have a measure \(c\) which is less than 5% (green cell in figure XX), we consider that these cells have a valid spatial configuration and we store the pair composed of their relative distance and the area of the surface of contact ie the area of the Voronoï face shared by both cell.

We will denote by an experiment \(i\) the aforementioned simulation with a set of \(N\) particle of generation \(g1=i\), \(g2=i+1\) and \(g3=i+2\). Each experiment thereby creates 6 sets of data called plots: a plot for every possible pair of generation ( \( g1/g1, g1/g2, g1/g3, g2/g2, g2/g3, g3/g3 \) ).

We performed 6 experiments with \(i\) belonging to the set \(\left [ 9, 14 \right ]\). Theses generations correspond to the zebrafish generations which can be mixed. It explains why the indices start at 9 but this fact is not relevant for the following study.

For each plot, \(numPoints(exp,g1,g2)\) points \(\left ( dist_{exp,g1,g2}(i), surf_{exp,g1,g2}(i) \right ), i \in \left [ 1,numPoints(exp,g1,g2) \right ]\) are collected, typically about 10000. We observe that, as expected, if most points are located below the distance \(R_i+R_j\), some of them exceed it.

The selected empirical law is written:

$$ A(r,R_i,R_j) = \begin{cases} a (r - c_{max} (R_i+R_j))^2 & \text{if }r< r_{max} (R_i+R_j) \\ 0 & \text{if }r\geq r_{max}(R_i+R_j) \end{cases}$$

We decide to use a monomial of degree 2 in order to scale as a surface. If the distance \(r\) between two double-sized neighbor particles \(p_i\) and \(p_j\) is doubled, the area of the surface of contact will quadruple.

$$A(2r,2R_i,2R_j) = 4 A(r,R_i,R_j)$$

To obtain the best pair of parameters \((a, c_{max})\), the discrepancy between the generated data and the empirical law is measured with the "sum of squared errors of prediction" (SSE) function. The smaller the SSE is, the better the law matches the data.

In general terms, the SSE is expressed:

$$SSE = \sum_{i = 1}^{n} \left ( y_i - f(x_i) \right )^2$$

In our case, the sample of variable is split in 6 experiments and 6 couple of particle types (\(g1/g2\)) per experiment, the ith observed variable \(y_i\) is the measured area of surface of contact \(surf_{exp,g1,g2}(i)\), and the value of the predicted variable \(f(x_i)\) is the value of the empirical law at the observated variable distance \(A(dist_{exp,g1,g2}(i),R_{g1},R_{g2})\). The SSE is written:

$$SSE (a,r_{max}) = \sum_{exp = 9}^{14} \sum_{g1 = exp}^{exp+2} \sum_{g2 = g1}^{exp+2} \left ( \frac{1}{< surf_{exp,g1,g2}>} \sum_{i = 1}^{numPoints(exp,g1,g2)} \left ( surf_{exp,g1,g2}(i) - a(dist_{exp,g1,g2}(i) - c_{max} (R_{g1} + R_{g2})) \right )^2 \right )$$

We explored exhaustively all the couple \((a,c_{max})\) until the 4th decimal. A heatmap of this exploration is vizualised below.

The best couple with four decimal is \((a=1.3697 , c_{max}=1.2414)\). These values are used throughout the rest of the manuscript.

Topological selection of neighbor particles

As exposed in the state of the art, a metric neighboring selection is not sufficient as it leads to collapsing simulation for high adhesive particle interactions. We propose to use a filtering of the metric list of neighbor with a topological criteria to avoid this drawback.

The delaunay triangulation solve this issue but is slow to compute because of its heavy data structure composed of tetraedra. Our solution is inspired by a similar neighboring method: the Gabriel graph.

In 2D, where the Delaunay triangulation imposes that no particle is inside the circumcircle of any triangle of the Delaunay triangulation, the Gabriel graph method imposes that no particle lie inside the circle whom diameter is a valid neighboring link.

We propose to extend the method in the 3D case: each particle pair \((p_i, p_j)\) is considered valid according to the Gabriel criteria if no other particle of the swarm lie inside the ball whose diameter is \(\left [ X_i X_j \right ] \).

Two cells of radius \(R_i\), \(R_j\) and of position \(X_i\) and \(X_j\) are considered neighbor according to the topological criteria iff no neighbor cell according to the metric criteria have their position inside the sphere of diameter \([X_i X_j]\).

$$\mathcal{N}_t (p_i) := \left \{ p_j \in \mathcal{N}_m (p_i) : \forall p_k \in \mathcal{N}_m (p_i), \left \| \frac{1}{2} ( X_i + X_j ) - X_k \right \| \geq \frac{1}{2} \left \| X_i - X_j \right \|\right \} $$

In fact, in the 2D case, the Gabriel graph is a subgraph of the Delaunay triangulation. Every pair of neighbor in a Gabriel graph are also neighbor according to the Delaunay criteria (see figure XX).

3.2.3. Interaction Potential

Two neighbor spheroidal particles with radii \(R_i\) and \(R_j\), separated with by a distance \(r\), will interact by means of forces \( \vec{F}_{i,j} \). In MECAGEN, these forces are decomposed in two parts:

  • Relaxing forces \(\vec{F_P}_{i,j}\), derived from an attraction/repulsion potential. They relax the swarm of particles toward an equilibrium state. The underlying principle is the surface maximization of every neighbor pair in the swarm.
  • Behavioral forces \(\vec{F_A}_{i,j}\), which move the swarm configuration away from the previous equilibrium. These behaviors are inspired by the protrusive activity of the cell.

Every pair of neighbor particles interaction potential share common features: repulsion at short distance, until an equilibrium distance \(c_{eq}(i) (R_i+R_j)\) and attraction afterwards, until the limit of the interaction field \(r_{max} \).

We established in the previous section how the maximum distance of interaction is determined, through an empirical study of the surface of contact between neighbor particles. Given this rule, we performed a two step strategy of selection of the neighbor particles.

As detailed in the section 3.1.X, the definition of the distance of equilibrium can be stated either explicitly or implicitly. In the implicit category, for example in the most advanced models in contact mechanics, the equilibrium distance results from a trade-off between the different biomechanical properties. We showed that theses models run up against major difficulty when multi-cellular interaction are considered.

We decide to explicitly express the distance of equilibrium of neighbor cells as the distance between their position in the densest arrangement of sphere pack. We assume that there is no significant volume is occupied by intercellular material. Otherwise, additional particle representing the extra-cellular material should be added (not presented here).

On a 2D plane, the densest arrangement of all possible circle packing is the hexagonal lattice (see figure XX). The ratio of the surface occupied by the disks to the surface containing the disks is equal to the ratio of the surface of a disk to the surface of an hexagon i.e. \(\frac{\pi}{ 2 \sqrt{3}} \simeq 0.9068997...\).

In 3D, Kepler conjecture states that the hexagonal close packing and face-centered cubic close packing arrangements of equally sized sphere have the greater density of all arrangement (proven by Thomas Hales in 1998). An hexagonal close packing arrangement is obtained by superposing alternatively two 2D hexagonal lattice of sphere with pattern ABAB... (see figure XX).

In this arrangement, each sphere touches 12 other spheres ie each particle has 12 neighbors (six particles on a 2D plane and three other particles on each of the surrounding planes). The space-filling tessellation which is dual to this arrangement has a tile called trapezo-rhombic dodecahedron (TRD) (see figure XX).

The atomic packing factor is the ratio of the volume of a sphere to the volume of the TDR and it is equal to \(\frac{\pi}{3\sqrt{2}} \simeq 0.74048... \). So, for a pair of cells of radii \(R_i = R_j=R\), their equilibrium distance is:

\( r_{eq} = {\frac{\pi}{3\sqrt{2}}} ^ {1/3} \left ( R_i + R_j \right ) \), with \(c_{eq} = {\frac{\pi}{3\sqrt{2}}} ^ {1/3} \simeq 0.904699895...\)

We extend the notion of distance of equilibrium in a pair of neighbor particle to the notion of a radius of equilibrium intrinsic to the particle. It means that each particle brings its own contribution to equilibrium distance between two neighbor cells.

We denote by \(r_{eq}(p_i)\) the radius of equilibrium of the particle \(p_i\). \(r_{eq}(p_i) = c_{eq}R_i\) (see figure XX)

In a pair of neighbor particle \(p_i\), \(p_j\), we get:

$$ r_{eq} = r_{eq}(p_i) + r_{eq}(p_j) $$

The intrisicness of the notion of equilibrium frees the requirement of the neighbor particles to have the same radius. We apply the equation XX ( \(r_{eq} = c_{eq} \left ( R_i + R_j \right )\) ) for every pair of neighbor particles, with no discrimination of size.

Attraction-Repulsion

As illustrated in figure XX, the attraction/repulsion potential must be repulsive for a distance below \(R_{eq}\), attractive between \(R_{eq}\) and \(R_{max}\) and null afterward.

We considered the simplest form of potential which catches the short range repulsion / long range attraction property. An spring force derived from the elastic potential.

The mechanical interaction between neighbor cell has been intensively studied by the biophysical scientific communauty. Roughly, the consensual hypothesis is that the driving mechanism is the intercellular surface tension. The two main components of the tension are the adhesion which tends to lower it and the cortical tension which acts antogonistically. The balance between these two components determine the behavior of the interaction: attraction or repulsion.

Another mechanism is also fundamental: cell preserve their volume. As we made the assumption that cell are spheroidal and that each cellular interaction is decorrelated from the other in its expression, we have to assume that the volume conservation principle participate to the repulsive part of the potential only. This allow us to introduce a discontinuity of the slope of force at the distance of equilibrium. Also, we will denote by adhesion the cumulated effect of effective surface adhesion and cortical tension.

To modulate the intensity of adhesion, we split the stiffness coefficient below and after equilibrium (see \(F_{linear}\) figure XX). A low (resp. high) adhesion coefficient \(w\) will induce a weak (resp. strong) attraction.

$$F_{\text{linear}} = \begin{cases} - rep (r-r_{eq}) & \text{if }r< r_{eq} \\ - w (r-r_{eq}) & \text{if }r\geq r_{eq} \end{cases}$$

Cells start to adhere when their relative distance is less than \(R_{max}\). Moreover, the intensity of the attraction can not be maximum at this point, a bell shape between \(R_{eq}\) and \(R_{max}\) suits more a realistic cellular interaction.

We obtain this shape by multiplying the linear force \(F_{linear}\) by the area of the surface of contact elaborated in X.X.X.X. The depth of the well is still controled by the adhesion coefficient \(w\) (see figure XX).

$$A(r,R_i,R_j) = \begin{cases} a (r - l (R_i+R_j))^2 & \text{if } r < l (R_i+R_j) \\ 0 & \text{if } r \geq l(R_i+R_j) \end{cases}$$

$$F = \begin{cases} - rep (r-r_{eq}) \cdot A(r,R_i,R_j) & \text{if }r < r_{eq} \\ - w (r-r_{eq}) \cdot A(r,R_i,R_j) & \text{if }r\geq r_{eq} \wedge r \leq r_{max} \\ 0 & \text{if } r \geq r_{max} \end{cases}$$

Cell specific behavioral forces

In addition to the equilibrium oriented attraction/repulsion forces, we introduce new forces which aim at expressing cell specialized biomechanical behaviors.

Cells manifest a wide variety of behavior across every species development. Multiple classification of these behaviors have been undertaken (find references). We will refer to our own cell behavior ontology (see CBO part).

Among these behaviors, we will introduce the one we believe to be the main driving force in our zebrafish early gastrulation study : cellular protrusion.

Protrusion

intro bio: keller axis schema

Our mechanical model abstracts cells as the microscopic level of our study. However, in order to explain our interpretation of the protrusive activity of a cell, we need to present this mechanism at the sub-cellular level. The following description reflects our understanding of protrusion based on multiple reading of the subject which is still subject of on-going intense research.

A cell protrusion is a cyclic activity. It is similar to the activity of the track of tracked vehicle except that, as gravity plays no part in cellular interactions, adhesion is specifically regulated to avoid a potential sliding of neighbor cells surfaces. The protrusive activity induces an intercalation of the cell between its neighbors.

One requirement for exerting protrusive activity is polarization. Cells need to have an asymmetrical distribution of internal substances to specify the direction in which they will protrude. We will not present here how this axis is determined (see coupling part XXX). The axis determines two regions of the cell belonging to its extremities: the poles of the cell. Each pole can host a protrusive activity for the cell. If only one pole is active, we call the cell monopolar and if both are active, bipolar.

At the subcellular level, the structure which serves as a base for protrusion is the cytoskeleton. The "cell's skeleton" denomination should evoke both the exoskeleton of insects and the skeleton of mammals. The external part is located at the cortex, the main molecules composing it are actin and myosin (here and in the following, proteins are denoted by their "family" name, a wide variety of cousins are hidden behind these global denomination). Internally, microfilaments, intermediate filaments and microtubules constitute the main units. To explain protrusion, we will focus on the acto-myosin cortex of the cell. Actin proteins are rangy molecules we may consider as our bones and myosins play the role of our muscles and ligaments relating bones. When associated together, they forms a both solid and flexible network which can resist internal and external pressure. Moreover, actin is a polymer which can disassemble itself at one side of its rangy structure and reassemble at the other side. This allows an active deformation of the acto-myosin network which, as shown is figure , is the driving mechanism of protrusion. The cells, attached all together by molecular bridges often belonging to the cadherin family, deforms their acto-myosin network in the direction of the polarization axis. A bulge is appearing at a pole, warding off the neighbor cells. However, no intercalation would be observed without an accurate regulation of the adhesion between the neighbor cells.

In addition to the regular adhesion between neighbor cell, special adhesion bonds called focal adhesion points appear at the surface of the protruding region of the cell. These bonds bear the extra load generated by the protrusive activity of the acto-myosin cortex. They become visible around the tip of the bulge and, as the cell is advancing, they maintain the spatial cohesion between the neighbor cytoskeleton. Without them, the cell would slip on the surface, or at least the efficiency of the protrusion would be massively reduced. The focal adhesion points disappear when the cell has advanced enough relatively to the bonded neighbor and the focal adhesion has left the protruding region of the cell surface.

The coupled action of acto-myosin cortex and the focal adhesion points form a treadmill originating at the tip of the protrusion. It induces a torque transmission between the protruding cell and each neighbor cell linked by focal adhesion points. The transfer of cell material in the bulge results from the "action" force generated by the acto-myosin cortex and the "reaction" force is exerted on the neighbor cell through the focal adhesion points. In some cases, an additional mechanism of cellular contraction at the back of the cell amplifies the "action" force (not presented here). As we suppose the distribution of focal adhesion points homogeneous on the surface of the cell, the quantity of torque transmitted between two neighbor cells scales with the area of their surface of contact \(A(r,R_i,R_j)\).

In our particle-based framework, the mathematical interpretation of this mechanism is the following: a cell \(p_i\) has a normalized polarization axis \({Up}_i\) that we consider given in this part. Among the neighbor cells belonging \(\mathcal{N}_t (p_i)\), we denote by \(P_{+}(\mathcal{N}_t (p_i))\) the sublist of neighbors which share a surface of contact on the positive pole of the cell.

$$ P_{+}(\mathcal{N}_t (p_i)) := \left \{ p_j \in \mathcal{N}_t (p_i) : \frac{( (X_j - X_i) \cdot {Up}_i)}{\left \| ( X_j - X_i ) \right \|} \geq \alpha_p \right \} $$

\(\alpha_{p}\) defines the relative size of the protrusion. Similarly, we denote by \(P_{-}(\mathcal{N}_t (p_i))\) the sublist of neighbors which share a surface of contact on the other pole of the cell.

For each neighbor \(p_j\) belonging \(P_{+}(\mathcal{N}_t (p_i))\), and by the Weak Law of Action and Reaction (Newton's third law), we introduce a pair of equal and opposite force applied on \(p_i\) and \(p_j\). The axis of the force is designed to roughly represent the profile of the surface of contact(see figXX). It is a linear combination of the protrusion axis \({Up}_i\) and of \(Up_{\perp i}(j)\) the axis orthogonal to \(Up_i\) which passes through \(p_j\). The constant terms of the linear combination set the profile of the surface of contact. It could be precisely described by a function of the angular position of the neighbor but we will let them constant these coefficient do not impact the outcome of the model (see example TODO???).

Eventually, the "action" forces resulting from each neighbors interaction are summed up to produce the resulting "action" force applied to protruding cell.
